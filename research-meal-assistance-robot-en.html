Research on the Development of Meal Assistance Robot Systems

A study on meal assistance robot systems for care and home environments, integrating food recognition, mouth recognition, manipulator control, delivery mechanisms, and psychological evaluation.

In Japan, with the progression of population aging and the shortage of caregiving personnel, meal assistance has become one of the more demanding tasks in real care settings. For people with restricted upper-limb function and those who require continuous support, it is important to realize meal assistance robot systems that are not only safe and stable, but also psychologically acceptable to users. Against this background, this research addresses meal support in care and home environments, aiming to reduce caregiver burden while improving user autonomy and comfort.

The scope of this research is not limited to a single mechanism or control strategy. Instead, it is structured as a total system that includes food recognition, mouth-position recognition, multi-joint manipulator control, delivery mechanism design, and subjective user evaluation. The work began with systems that handled commercially available lunch boxes and gradually established an integrated process for food recognition and manipulator-based transfer. It then evolved toward more specific mechanisms, such as toothpick piercing and spoon-based food delivery to the mouth, in order to improve the stable handling of solid food items. In addition, the research has explored the use of an independent delivery module near the user’s mouth, which reduces the psychological anxiety that may arise when the main manipulator approaches the face directly, thereby improving both safety and acceptance.

To realize these functions, the system combines ROS, MATLAB, a RealSense camera, and a multi-joint manipulator. Visual recognition is used to obtain the positions of food and the user’s mouth, and the manipulator then performs food acquisition, transfer, and offering based on those positions. For different food shapes and offering actions, multiple end-effector designs and motion strategies are compared in order to find an appropriate balance between operational stability, usability, and human friendliness. Furthermore, the research does not focus only on the engineering question of whether food can be brought to the mouth. It also pays close attention to the psychological impression experienced by users in response to delivery trajectories, approach speed, and motion style. For this reason, subjective evaluations are conducted from perspectives such as perceived safety, predictability, and comfort, and these evaluations are incorporated into the design of the entire system.

In terms of its development process, this project has progressed step by step from system foundation building to visual recognition, mechanism design, control strategies, and psychological evaluation. Through these efforts, the research aims to establish a meal assistance robot system that can be used in real care and home environments. Such a system must not only be technically feasible, but also gentle, reassuring, and naturally acceptable to human users.
