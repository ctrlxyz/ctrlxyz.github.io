Functional Electrical Stimulation for Gait Assistance in Knee Osteoarthritis

This research investigates active gait assistance using functional electrical stimulation for patients with knee osteoarthritis. The goal is to improve gait patterns and reduce joint loading while developing a practical system suitable for long-term home use.

Background

Background

In Japan’s super-aging society, musculoskeletal disorders are a major cause of support and nursing care needs. Knee osteoarthritis is especially common and is associated with pain, varus deformity, lateral instability, and decreased muscle strength, all of which contribute to deteriorated gait function.

Conventional aids such as canes, knee braces, and insoles provide mainly passive support. In contrast, functional electrical stimulation offers the possibility of actively modulating muscle contraction timing and promoting more desirable motion patterns.

Approach

Approach

Gait Analysis

We quantitatively evaluate gait using 3D motion analysis and force plates, focusing on knee varus angle, external knee adduction moment, ground reaction force, walking speed, step length, cadence, and single-leg support time.

FES Intervention

Electrical stimulation is applied to muscles around the knee during appropriate gait phases in order to support stance-phase muscle activity and guide walking toward a more desirable pattern.

Active Assistance

Unlike passive orthotic devices, this approach actively induces contraction of target muscles in synchrony with natural timing, potentially improving both gait posture and muscle strength.

Extended Application

We also consider severe bilateral cases and pre/post total knee arthroplasty alignment changes to evaluate the adaptability of the stimulation system.

Experiment

Experimental Plan

The system will first be refined and tested with healthy participants to confirm safety and basic performance, followed by gait experiments with patients with knee osteoarthritis to validate the proposed stimulation control strategy.

Goal

Final Goal

Our long-term goal is to develop a compact, lightweight, portable gait assistance device for home use that reduces patient burden, supports rehabilitation continuity, and contributes to care prevention and welfare engineering.
