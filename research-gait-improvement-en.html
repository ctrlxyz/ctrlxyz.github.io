Gait Improvement for Patients with Knee Osteoarthritis

This study focuses on improving gait in patients with knee osteoarthritis through the development of a functional electrical stimulation based walking assistance device and the biomechanical validation of its effectiveness.

In Japan, the declining birthrate and shrinking population, together with the rapid increase in older adults, have led to a super-aging society, and the number of elderly people who fall within the category of locomotive syndrome and are at high risk of requiring support or nursing care because of bone and joint diseases has been increasing sharply. The population requiring nursing care continues to rise and had reached 6.58 million by the end of March 2018. Among the causes of needing support or long-term care, fractures and falls ranked fourth at 12.5%, and joint diseases ranked fifth at 10.2%; together they accounted for 22.7%, exceeding dementia, which ranked first at 18.7%. For this reason, addressing bone and joint disorders, which form the basis of mobility, is highly important from the standpoint of care prevention and health promotion, and has been designated as a major issue by Japan’s Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare.

Bone and joint disorders that impair human motor function arise from various causes. They include fractures caused by strong external force that disrupts bone continuity, rheumatoid arthritis in which articular cartilage destruction progresses, and osteoarthritis in which articular cartilage degenerates. Among these disorders, osteoarthritis has the largest number of patients. It is a condition in which loading causes compression and wear of the articular cartilage, concentrating mechanical stress at the contact areas between bones and leading to selective deformation of the joint surface. In osteoarthritis of the lower-limb joints, joint instability occurs during activities such as walking, increasing compression, wear, and shear force at the joint surface. As a result, cartilage degeneration progresses, pain worsens, and walking and standing up become difficult. In addition, worsening pain in the lower-limb joints reduces activity levels, which leads to atrophy of lower-limb muscles, particularly the quadriceps femoris and triceps surae. This in turn makes joint control during walking unstable and promotes further joint degeneration. The disease thus repeats a vicious cycle. Among the forms of osteoarthritis, knee osteoarthritis in particular continues to increase with population aging. In Japan, the number of patients is estimated at 25.3 million. As shown in the figure, severe deformity of the knee often progresses, and X-ray images frequently reveal disappearance of the articular cartilage. Therefore, preventing further progression of joint deformity is one of the key issues in treatment.

In knee osteoarthritis, it is important to implement rehabilitation from an early stage to prevent progression and improve the patient’s quality of life. In reality, however, because of social security costs and shortages of medical staff, many cases are managed only with medication and patches, making early-stage prevention of progression difficult. In addition, because many patients are older adults, the increase in single-person households means that regular hospital visits are often difficult. Since February 2020, the impact of COVID-19 has reduced Japan’s estimated medical expenditure by 8.5%. This indicates that, due to restrictions such as avoiding hospital visits and suspending surgeries, the number of patients unable to receive appropriate medical care and rehabilitation increased sharply.

Against this background, conventional walking support for knee osteoarthritis has consisted of canes, knee braces, and insoles intended to correct knee alignment. These supports are primarily intended to compensate for bodily function and are not approaches that actively draw out biological responses. Although many medical approaches to knee osteoarthritis have been attempted, comparatively fewer have been pursued from an engineering perspective. In particular, there have been very few attempts to actively elicit biological responses. Therefore, the development of devices based on engineering viewpoints is important, and there is an urgent need for equipment that does not require frequent hospital visits and can be used continuously at home with ease.

As noted above, most older adults with knee osteoarthritis who need walking support currently rely only on welfare devices such as canes, supporters, and insoles. Although these measures have produced certain benefits, in disorders accompanied by weakening of lower-limb muscle strength they mainly protect the affected region and do not directly act on the muscles, making it difficult to suppress progression of disease-related muscle weakness. However, no walking assistance device has yet been established for knee osteoarthritis that directly acts on muscles and aims to transform walking motion through muscle contraction. The development of such a device is therefore an important research issue. A compact walking support device that can be used at home would also be beneficial because it could reduce both physical and psychological burden on patients and make it easier for them to engage in walking practice. Home-based use may reduce the number of hospital visits, which in turn may help lessen the burden on frontline medical staff.

Several compact walking support devices have been developed so far. Examples include improved versions of conventional knee braces such as OA Fantasy, treadmill-based gait training systems such as Welwalk, and power-suit type devices such as HAL. Knee braces are simple and advantageous in terms of cost, but their effect on the muscle contractions that control the body is limited. Treadmill-based gait training systems can be used even in severe cases, but they are expensive and somewhat large, making them unsuitable for home use. Power suits are wearable and relatively lightweight, but issues remain in terms of fitting judgment and cost for personal use. Under these conditions, if one seeks a device that is small, inexpensive, and simple to use, functional electrical stimulation emerges as one of the promising candidates. Functional electrical stimulation is intended to reconstruct lost motor function by continuing to apply electrical stimulation to the necessary muscles at the proper timing so that movement can be carried out. Several existing FES devices for gait already exist, particularly for foot drop after stroke, where electrical stimulation is applied during swing phase to prevent drop foot and improve walking. Although the number of implementations is increasing in Japan, there are still no established cases in which FES has been introduced for the purpose of transforming gait in knee osteoarthritis, a musculoskeletal disorder, and most patients continue to be followed up only with conventional welfare devices.

In walking with knee osteoarthritis, varus deformity, or bow-leg alignment, often causes lateral instability of the knee joint. This increases mechanical stress in the medial compartment of the knee, and long-term wear of the medial joint surface promotes cartilage degeneration, causing pain and making walking difficult. At the same time, atrophy progresses in muscles centered on the quadriceps femoris, one of the largest muscle groups in the human lower limb, and the resulting decline in muscle output tends to further promote joint instability, making movement increasingly difficult. Decline in mobility may also lead to reduced whole-body endurance and, in some cases, eventual bedridden status. For this reason, it is important to intervene before cartilage degeneration in the knee joint advances further.

Knee osteoarthritis causes a wide range of problems in daily life. In particular, walking, which is fundamental to activities of daily living, affects many aspects of life. In this study, we therefore first use a three-dimensional motion analysis system and force plates, which allow highly accurate motion analysis, to analyze the knee joint movements that become problematic during walking in patients with bilateral knee osteoarthritis from a biomechanical perspective. Specifically, gait analysis is performed in patients with bilateral knee osteoarthritis in order to clarify the characteristic features that make walking problematic in this disorder.

Next, we develop a functional electrical stimulation device aimed at reducing the mechanical stress applied to the medial side of the knee from early to mid-stance, which has been identified as a major feature of knee osteoarthritis, by inducing a change in walking motion during gait. By assisting muscle contraction during the stance phase, where stronger muscular activity is required, the proposed approach aims to transform movement into a more desirable pattern and reduce knee joint load. Unlike existing devices, this approach applies electrical stimulation in synchrony with biological muscle contraction, making it possible to elicit actual contraction of the target muscles and potentially achieve a muscle strengthening effect as well.

After that, the proposed device is tested and improved repeatedly using a healthy-subject model, and once safety and effectiveness are confirmed, functional electrical stimulation is applied during walking in patients with knee osteoarthritis. In the experiments, a three-dimensional motion analysis system and two force plates are used to quantitatively evaluate gait from a biomechanical viewpoint based on joint angles, joint moments, ground reaction forces, and gait parameters such as walking speed, cadence, stride length, and single-leg support time. These results are then used to verify the effectiveness of the proposed stimulation control method and its effects on the subjects.

In addition, even when functional electrical stimulation is applied to gait in severe bilateral knee osteoarthritis, sufficient improvement may not be obtained, and many cases eventually require total knee arthroplasty as a final treatment option. For this reason, the study also examines cases in which bilateral knee osteoarthritis progresses to unilateral total knee arthroplasty, causing a rapid change in lower-limb alignment over a short period. In such cases, the effects on both the operated and non-operated sides, as well as the applicability of the functional electrical stimulation device, are investigated.

As the final concept of this research on a compact walking support device for home use, we aim to create a lightweight and portable system. Such a device should be safe for anyone to use and is expected to contribute to improving walking function in patients with knee osteoarthritis.
