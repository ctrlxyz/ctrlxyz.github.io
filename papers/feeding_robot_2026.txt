Development of a Meal Assistance Robot Based on Spoon-Offering 
Motions and Its Psychological Evaluation on Users 
 
Daiju Fujiia, Satoru Shibataa, Shenglin Mua,* 
 
a Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Ehime University 
3 Bunkyo-cho, Matsuyama, Japan 
 
*Corresponding Author: mu.shenglin.du@ehime-u.ac.jp 
 
 
Abstract 
This study proposes an improved feeding assistance 
system integrating a multi -joint manipulator with a 
dedicated food -transfer mechanism to enhance operational 
safety and user acceptance. By separating food transport 
from the primary manipulator, the system reduces 
psychological discomfort associated with direct utensil 
approach. To determine motion strategies that ensure both 
safety and psychological comfort, three food -transfer 
motions were designed and comparatively evaluated. A 
psychological assessment experiment using the rating scale 
method was conducted to quantify user perception. The 
findings provide practical design insights for developing 
safe, reliable, and psychologically acceptable meal 
assistance robots for real-world caregiving applications. 
Keywords: assistive robotics, meal assistance system, 
psychological evaluation. 
1. Introduction 
Japan is currently facing a super-aging society in which 
the proportion of individuals requiring nursing care 
continues to increase due to population aging, while the 
number of caregivers is declining because of a decreasing 
birthrate (1-2). Meal assistance, particularly for individuals 
with upper-limb impairments, requires substantial caregiver 
time and represents a significant physical and psychological 
burden. Therefore, robotic systems capable of supporting 
feeding tasks are increasingly needed in clinical and 
home-care environments. 
In our previous work, a meal assistance robot 
incorporating a toothpick -based piercing motion and a 
spoon-offering motion was proposed  (3). However, because 
the toothpick was directly transported to the user’s mouth, 
the system induced psychological discomfort and fear in 
some users, raising concerns regarding safety and 
acceptability. 
To address this issue, this study proposes an improved 
meal assistance system consisting of a multi -joint 
manipulator and an additional dedicated food -transfer 
mechanism that transports food to the vicinity of the user’s 
mouth. By separating the food -delivery function from the 
main manipulator, the system aims to enhance operational 
safety and reduce psychological burden. 
Furthermore, to identify motion strategies that ensure 
high safety while minimizing psychological stress, three 
different food -transfer motions were designed and 
experimentally compared. A psychological evaluation 
experiment was conducted using the rating scale method to 
quantitatively assess user perception. The results provide 
design insights for safe and psychologically acceptable 
meal assistance robots suitable for practical industrial and 
caregiving applications. 
2. Meal Assistance System Configuration 
2.1 Overall System Architecture 
Figure 1 illustrates the overall configuration of the 
proposed meal assistance system. The system is designed to 
support feeding tasks for users seated in front of the robot. 
A multi-joint robotic arm (Open Manipulator) mounted on a 
base is positioned facing the user, and food items are placed 
on a platform integrated with the base. 
The control architecture is implemented on a control 
PC integrating the Robot Operating System (ROS) and 
MATLAB, as shown in Fig. 2. A depth -sensing stereo 
vision camera (Intel RealSense D435i) is mounted on the 
robotic arm to acquire three -dimensional point cloud data. 
Using this 3D information, the positions of both the food 
and the user’s mouth are recognized through a 
convolutional neural network (CNN) -based detection 
algorithm. The calculated coordinates are then used to 
control the manipulator for food handling and delivery. 
The system is designed to achieve stable and precise 
food transfer while ensuring user safety and psychological 
comfort (4). 
2.2 Manipulator-Based Feeding Module 
In the initial system design, the articulated robotic arm 
was responsible for both grasping and delivering the food to 
the user’s mouth. A toothpick attached to the end effector 
was used to pierce the food item, and the manipulator 
transported it directly toward the user. 
RealSense D435i provided depth information that 
enabled accurate centroid estimation of the food item. 
Based on the estimated centroid, the end effector was 
positioned precisely to achieve stable piercing. After 
grasping, the manipulator moved along a predefined 
trajectory to deliver the food to the user’s mouth. 
Although this approach achieved stable transport, 
directly moving a sharp object such as a toothpick toward 
the user’s face raised safety concerns and induced 
psychological discomfort. The perceived risk and fear 
associated with this motion limited user acceptance, 
highlighting the need for a safer and more psychologically 
acceptable delivery strategy. 
To mitigate this issue, a spoon -equipped end effector 
was introduced. However, even with a spoon, directly 
transporting food solely using the manipulator could still 
cause anxiety due to the approach trajectory and proximity 
to the face. Therefore, a structural redesign separating 
grasping and delivery functions was proposed  as shown in 
Fig. 1.

2.3 Proposed Food-Transfer Mechanism 
To enhance safety and reduce psychological burden, a 
dedicated food -transfer mechanism was newly designed 
and integrated with the existing manipulator system. The 
manipulator performs food recognition and grasping, while 
the transfer mechanism is responsible for transporting the 
food from the grasping position to the vicinity of the user’s 
mouth. 
The external appearance of the transfer mechanism is 
shown in Fig. 3. The mechanism consists of two 
subsystems: a forward extension mechanism and a vertical 
lifting mechanism. By combining these two motions, 
flexible and psychologically adaptive transfer strategies can 
be realized. 
The forward extension mechanism was designed based 
on a slider–crank concept. It consists of a spoon -shaped end 
effector, a servo motor, a driving link connecting the motor 
to the end effector, and an intermediate link. These 
components are mounted on the upper platform of the 
lifting mechanism. When the servo motor rotates, the spoon 
carrying the food is pushed forward. The extension distance 
can be controlled by adjusting the servo rotation angle. 
The lifting mechanism is constructed using a linkage 
system driven by a servo motor. By rotating the motor shaft 
connected to the linkage, vertical motion is generated while 
maintaining the horizontal orientation of the extension 
mechanism. The height of the spoon can be precisely 
adjusted by controlling the motor angle, ensuring stable 
vertical positioning during delivery. 
By separating the grasping and transport functions, the 
system reduces the direct approach of the manipulator 
toward the user’s face. This structural design enhances 
operational safety and enables the implementation of 
multiple motion strategies aimed at improving 
psychological acceptance. The detailed motion strategies 
are described in the following section. 
3. Motion Strategies 
This section describes the three proposed food -transfer 
motions implemented using the developed transfer 
mechanism.

followed by the forward extension motion. The food is 
initially raised to the height of the user’s mouth and then 
delivered forward. Because the food reaches the appropriate 
vertical position before being extended toward the user, this 
motion allows the user to more easily perceive and adjust 
the distance to the approaching food. This stepwise 
movement is expected to improve spatial predictability and 
reduce uncertainty during feeding assistance. 
B. Transfer Motion 2 
Transfer Motion 2 is shown in Fig. 5. In this strategy, 
the lifting and forward extension motions are executed 
simultaneously. By combining both movements, the food is 
transported along a shorter, more direct trajectory toward 
the user’s mouth. This motion enables delivery within the 
minimum transfer distance, potentially reducing operation 
time and improving task efficiency. 
C. Transfer Motion 3 
Transfer Motion 3 is illustrated in Fig. 6. In this 
approach, the forward extension motion is performed first, 
followed by the lifting motion. Because the extension is 
executed at a position farther from the user’s face, the 
subsequent upward movement occurs after the spoon has 
already advanced. This motion is designed to reduce 
psychological discomfort by avoiding a direct upward 
approach near the user’s face, thereby minimizing 
perceived fear or anxiety during food delivery. 
4. Psychological Evaluation Using the Rating 
Scale Method 
A psychological evaluation experiment was conducted 
using a five -point semantic differential scale with 14 
participants in their twenties (average height approximately 
170 cm). The participants were seated facing the transfer 
mechanism approximately 20 cm from the table and were 
asked to experience each transfer motion. 
After each motion was completed, participants 
evaluated the motion using a five -point scale for six 
adjective pairs shown in Figs. 7 –9. During the experiment, 
a food model (fried chicken sample) was placed on the 
spoon. The mechanism performed a forward extension of 
130 mm and a lifting motion from the table height to 350 
mm. 
The results of the psychological evaluation are 
presented in Figs. 7 –9. In each figure, filled circles 
represent the mean values, and error bars indicate standard 
deviations. 
For transfer motion 1 (Fig. 7), high ratings were 
obtained for the adjective pairs “safe –dangerous” and 
“predictable–unexpected.” In addition, relatively high 
scores were also observed for “like –dislike.” The mean 
scores for “calm–surprising” and “human-like–mechanical” 
were below the neutral value of 3. These results indicate 
that while the motion was perceived as safe and predictable, 
it was also regarded as somewhat surprising and 
mechanical rather than human-like. 
 For transfer motion 2 (Fig. 8), all evaluation items 
received mean scores above the neutral value of 3. In 
particular, the item “like–dislike” showed the highest score, 
indicating a strong overall preference for this motion.
